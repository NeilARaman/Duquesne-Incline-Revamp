Early History

The Duquesne Incline is one of those which follows very closely the tracks of an early coal hoist. Old newspapers indicate the existence of such a conveyance as early as 1854. Residents apparently referred to it as "Kirk Lewis' incline" and located it on the present site of The Duquesne Incline's Upper Station.

Grandview Avenue was then the High Street, and the area was just beginning to be converted from farmland to homesites. This pleasantly-situated acreage was an excellent area to absorb some of the expanding population of the city, but the problem of easy access was still to be solved.

Although plans for a freight and passenger incline are known to have existed much earlier, financial backing was exceedingly hard to find--even the modest sum of $47,000--and actual construction was delayed. Eventually, difficulties were overcome and the Incline was built. It was opened to the public May 20, 1877.

The Duquesne Incline shortly after opening in 1877

Timeline of Key Events

1877

The Duquesne Incline opened to the public on May 20th, designed and built by Samuel Diescher for Kirk Bigham and Associates.

1888

The incline structure was rebuilt entirely of iron. Originally part wood and part iron, the new structure featured five-foot riveted girders in spans of sixty feet.

1932

Converted from steam power to electrical operation, modernizing the mechanism while preserving the original cars.

1962

The Duquesne Incline was closed due to declining ridership and economic challenges.

1963

Local residents formed a group to rescue and restore the incline, successfully reopening it to the public.

1964

The Society for the Preservation of the Duquesne Heights Incline was formally established to maintain and operate the incline.

Present

The Duquesne Incline continues to operate as both a working transportation system and a historic attraction, maintained by the Society and owned by the Port Authority of Allegheny County.

Technical Specifications

Structure

The total length is 793 feet, the grade is 58.5 percent or 30.5 degrees, and the total rise is 400 feet. The gauge is five feet, the double trackway is twenty feet wide.

Cars

The original wooden cars were built by J. G. Brill and Company of Philadelphia. Two cars operate in a counterbalanced system, passing each other in the middle of the incline.

Operation

Originally powered by steam, the incline was converted to electrical operation in the 1930s while preserving the historical mechanism and appearance.
